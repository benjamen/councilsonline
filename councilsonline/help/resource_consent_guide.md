## Getting Started

### What is a Resource Consent?

A Resource Consent is permission to do something that would otherwise contravene the rules in a district plan, regional plan, or the Resource Management Act 1991 (RMA).

### Do I need a Resource Consent?

You may need a resource consent if your proposed activity:
- Doesn't comply with district plan rules
- Involves subdivision of land
- Requires a discharge permit (stormwater, wastewater)
- Needs a water permit (take, use, dam, or divert water)
- Requires a coastal permit (structures or activities in the coastal marine area)

**Always check your district plan first** - many activities are permitted and don't require consent.

- **Zone**: Check your district plan (e.g., Residential, Rural, Commercial)

- **Brief Description**: 1-2 sentence summary
- **Detailed Description**: Comprehensive explanation of what you're proposing

### Step 4: Resource Consent Details *(This step only appears for RC applications)*

#### Consent Type
Select the primary consent type required:
- **Land Use**: Building or using land in a way that doesn't comply with district plan rules
- **Subdivision**: Dividing land into separate titles
- **Discharge Permit**: Discharging contaminants (e.g., stormwater)
- **Water Permit**: Taking, using, damming, or diverting water
- **Coastal Permit**: Activities in the coastal marine area

#### Activity Status
*See detailed guide below*

#### Assessment of Environmental Effects (AEE)
*See detailed guide below*

#### Planning Assessment
Explain how your proposal aligns with:
- District Plan objectives and policies
- Regional Plan provisions
- Part 2 of the RMA (purpose and principles)

#### Alternatives Considered
Describe what other options you considered and why you chose this approach.

#### Mitigation Measures
Explain what you'll do to **avoid**, **remedy**, or **mitigate** adverse effects.

### Step 5: Documents
Upload all supporting documents:
- Site plans and drawings
- Engineering reports
- Specialist assessments
- Written approvals from affected parties
- Cultural Impact Assessment (if applicable)

### Step 6: Review & Submit
- Review all information for accuracy
- Accept terms and conditions
- Submit application

## RMA Activity Status Guide

### What is Activity Status?

Activity status determines how council processes your application and what grounds they can use to decline it. **Check your district plan zone rules** to determine the status of your activity.

### Activity Status Types

#### Permitted Activity
- ‚úÖ **No consent required**
- Activity fully complies with district plan rules
- Can proceed without an application

#### Controlled Activity
- ‚úÖ **Consent must be granted**
- Council cannot decline the application
- Council sets conditions to manage effects
- Focus is on appropriate conditions, not whether to grant consent
- ‚è±Ô∏è Usually processed within 20 working days

#### Restricted Discretionary Activity
- ‚ö†Ô∏è **Council has limited discretion**
- Council can only decline on specific matters listed in the plan
- Council's consideration restricted to matters specified in the plan
- ‚è±Ô∏è Usually processed within 20 working days (non-notified)

#### Discretionary Activity
- ‚ö†Ô∏è **Council has full discretion**
- Council can grant or decline based on all relevant matters
- May require public notification depending on effects
- More detailed assessment required
- ‚è±Ô∏è Processing time varies (20-130 working days depending on notification)

#### Non-Complying Activity
- üö´ **Doesn't comply with district plan**
- Requires special justification
- Must demonstrate proposal is not contrary to plan objectives/policies
- Higher scrutiny and longer processing times
- Strong possibility of public notification
- üí° **Consider pre-application meeting with council**

#### Prohibited Activity
- ‚ùå **Cannot be consented under any circumstances**
- Council cannot grant consent
- Activity not permitted by the plan
- Consider alternative approaches or locations

### How to Determine Activity Status

1. Check your property's **zone** (Residential, Rural, Commercial, etc.)
2. Look up your activity in the **district plan zone rules**
3. Check if you comply with all relevant standards (height, setbacks, site coverage, etc.)
4. If you breach a standard, check what activity status applies

**Need help?** Contact the council planning team or consider hiring a planning consultant.

## Assessment of Environmental Effects (AEE)

### What is an AEE?

The Assessment of Environmental Effects (AEE) is a detailed description of your proposal's actual and potential effects on the environment, as required by **Schedule 4 of the RMA**.

### What to Include

#### 1. Description of the Proposal
- What you're proposing to do
- Where it will occur
- When it will occur
- How it will be undertaken

#### 2. Effects on People
Consider and describe:
- **Privacy**: Will the proposal overlook neighboring properties?
- **Sunlight Access**: Will it cast shadows on neighbors?
- **Visual Amenity**: How will it look from surrounding properties?
- **Noise**: Construction noise and ongoing operational noise
- **Traffic**: Additional vehicle movements
- **Property Values**: Potential impact on surrounding properties

#### 3. Physical Effects
Consider and describe:
- **Traffic & Parking**: Vehicle trips, parking provision, road safety
- **Stormwater**: How will stormwater be managed?
- **Infrastructure**: Water, wastewater, electricity demands
- **Earthworks**: Soil disturbance, sediment control
- **Construction**: Dust, vibration, working hours

#### 4. Ecosystem Effects
Consider and describe:
- **Indigenous Vegetation**: Native trees, shrubs, plants
- **Habitat Values**: Wildlife corridors, bird nesting sites
- **Watercourses**: Streams, wetlands, water quality
- **Soil Quality**: Contamination, erosion
- **Natural Character**: Modification of natural features

#### 5. Cultural/Heritage Effects
Consider and describe:
- **Heritage Buildings**: Listed heritage sites nearby
- **Archaeological Sites**: Pre-1900 sites
- **Sites of Significance to MƒÅori**: Waahi tapu, historic sites
- **Cultural Landscapes**: Areas of cultural importance

### Tips for Writing an AEE

‚úÖ **Be specific and factual** - Use measurements, distances, and data

‚úÖ **Be honest about effects** - Don't downplay genuine concerns

‚úÖ **Explain mitigation** - For each effect, describe how you'll address it

‚úÖ **Use specialist reports** - Reference technical assessments where relevant

‚úÖ **Consider cumulative effects** - What happens combined with other activities?

‚úÖ **Scale matters** - Level of detail should match the scale of effects:
   - Minor effects = brief AEE
   - Significant effects = detailed AEE with specialist reports

## Affected Parties

### Who are Affected Parties?

Affected parties are people or organizations that may be adversely affected by your proposal. This typically includes:
- Adjacent property owners
- Neighbors within sight/sound of the proposal
- Owners of properties that share access
- Utility providers
- Community groups

### Written Approvals

If you can obtain **written approval** from affected parties:
- Council may process your application without notification
- Faster processing (20 working days instead of 70+ for notified)
- Lower cost (no notification fees)
- Reduces risk of submissions in opposition

### How to Get Written Approvals

1. **Identify affected parties** - Who might be adversely affected?
2. **Prepare information pack**:
   - Plans and drawings
   - Description of proposal
   - Explanation of potential effects
   - Mitigation measures
3. **Meet in person** - Discuss the proposal face-to-face
4. **Address concerns** - Be prepared to make reasonable changes
5. **Get it in writing** - Use council's written approval form (if available)

### What if Someone Won't Give Approval?

- Document your consultation efforts
- Explain in your AEE what concerns they raised
- Describe what mitigation you've proposed to address concerns
- Council will consider their views in the decision

## Specialist Reports

### When are Specialist Reports Required?

Specialist reports provide technical expertise on specific aspects of your proposal. They're typically required when:
- Effects are complex or technical in nature
- District plan requires them for certain activities
- Council requests them during processing

### Common Specialist Report Types

#### Traffic Assessment
**When needed**: Significant increase in vehicle movements, changes to access, parking concerns

**Prepared by**: Traffic engineer

**Includes**: Trip generation, sight distances, parking provision, road safety

---

#### Geotechnical Report
**When needed**: Steep sites, unstable ground, earthworks, retaining walls

**Prepared by**: Geotechnical engineer

**Includes**: Soil analysis, stability assessment, foundation recommendations

---

#### Acoustic Assessment
**When needed**: Noise-generating activities (industrial, entertainment, mechanical equipment)

**Prepared by**: Acoustic consultant

**Includes**: Noise modeling, compliance with district plan noise limits, mitigation

---

#### Stormwater Design
**When needed**: Impermeable surfaces, changes to drainage patterns, large sites

**Prepared by**: Civil engineer

**Includes**: Stormwater calculations, detention design, water quality treatment

---

#### Ecological Assessment
**When needed**: Indigenous vegetation removal, wetlands, streams, protected species

**Prepared by**: Ecologist

**Includes**: Ecological survey, habitat assessment, biodiversity impacts, mitigation

---

#### Heritage Impact Assessment
**When needed**: Works to heritage buildings or in heritage areas

**Prepared by**: Heritage architect or consultant

**Includes**: Historical research, architectural assessment, impact analysis

---

#### Archaeological Assessment
**When needed**: Ground disturbance where pre-1900 sites may exist

**Prepared by**: Archaeologist (with Heritage NZ authority required)

**Includes**: Site survey, archaeological potential, recommendations

---

#### Landscape Assessment
**When needed**: Significant visual change, Outstanding Natural Landscapes

**Prepared by**: Landscape architect

**Includes**: Landscape character assessment, visual effects, mitigation

---

### How to Add Specialist Reports

1. Click **"Add Report"** in the Specialist Reports section
2. Select report type from the list (or enter custom type)
3. Enter specialist/consultant name
4. Enter date prepared
5. Upload the actual report file in the Documents step

## Iwi Consultation

### Why is Iwi Consultation Important?

The Treaty of Waitangi and the RMA require consideration of:
- Relationship of MƒÅori with ancestral lands, water, sites, waahi tapu
- Cultural and spiritual values
- Protection of taonga (treasures)

### When is Consultation Required?

Iwi consultation is important when your proposal:
- Involves earthworks or ground disturbance
- Affects watercourses, wetlands, or coastal areas
- Is near sites of significance to MƒÅori
- Involves indigenous vegetation
- May affect cultural or spiritual values

### How to Undertake Iwi Consultation

1. **Identify relevant iwi/hap≈´**:
   - Contact council for iwi contact details
   - Multiple iwi may have interests in your area

2. **Make contact early**:
   - Email or call iwi/hap≈´ representatives
   - Request a meeting to discuss your proposal

3. **Prepare information**:
   - Site location and legal description
   - Description of proposal
   - Plans and drawings
   - Potential cultural effects

4. **Listen and respond**:
   - Understand their concerns and values
   - Be prepared to modify your proposal
   - Propose mitigation measures

5. **Document the process**:
   - Record who you consulted with
   - Summarize their response
   - Explain what you've done in response

### Cultural Impact Assessment (CIA)

A CIA is a formal report prepared by iwi/hap≈´ that assesses cultural impacts of your proposal.

**When needed**:
- Complex or sensitive proposals
- Significant cultural effects
- Requested by iwi or council

**Who prepares it**: Iwi/hap≈´ representatives (you usually pay for this)

**What it includes**: Cultural values, effects assessment, recommendations, conditions

**How to upload**: Use the file upload field in the Iwi Consultation section (PDF or Word, max 10MB)

## Statutory Timeframes

### How Long Does Consent Take?

Processing time depends on the activity status and whether your application is notified.

#### Non-Notified Consents
| Activity Status | Timeframe |
|----------------|-----------|
| Controlled | 20 working days |
| Restricted Discretionary | 20 working days |
| Discretionary | 20 working days |

#### Limited Notified Consents
- **Minimum**: 70 working days
- Notification to affected parties only
- Submissions period (20 working days)
- Hearing if required
- Decision preparation

#### Publicly Notified Consents
- **Minimum**: 100-130 working days
- Public notification period
- Open submissions (20 working days)
- Hearing almost always required
- Decision preparation
- Possible Environment Court appeal

### Statutory Clock

The **statutory clock** tracks council's processing time and only runs during active processing:

#### Clock Starts When:
‚úÖ Application is **acknowledged** and accepted as complete

#### Clock Stops When:
‚è∏Ô∏è **Request for Information (RFI)** is issued to you
- Clock pauses while you gather additional information
- Can stop multiple times during processing

#### Clock Resumes When:
‚ñ∂Ô∏è You **provide the requested information**

### How to Track Your Application

When viewing your application:
- **Days Elapsed**: Working days council has used
- **Days Remaining**: Working days left before statutory deadline
- **Clock Status**: Running or stopped (RFI issued)
- **Progress Bar**: Visual indicator of time used

**Note**: Working days exclude weekends and public holidays.

## Common Questions

### Q: Do I need a planning consultant?

**A: Not required, but often helpful for**:
- Complex applications (discretionary, non-complying)
- Technical planning assessments
- Understanding district plan rules
- Navigating the consenting process
- Notified applications (submissions/hearings)

Simple controlled or restricted discretionary applications can often be done yourself.

---

### Q: How much does a resource consent cost?

**A: Costs vary based on**:
- **Application fee**: Set by council (usually $500-$5,000)
- **Processing charges**: Staff time (typically $150-200/hour)
- **Notification costs**: If application is notified ($1,000-$5,000+)
- **Specialist reports**: $1,500-$15,000+ depending on type
- **Consultant fees**: If you hire a planner ($2,000-$20,000+)

Total cost range: $2,000-$50,000+ depending on complexity.

---

### Q: Can I save my application and come back later?

**A: Yes!**
- Your application auto-saves as a draft
- You can exit and return anytime
- Click "Save Draft" to manually save
- Find your draft applications on your Dashboard

---

### Q: What if my application is declined?

**A: You have several options**:
1. **Modify and resubmit** - Address council's concerns
2. **Request reconsideration** - If you believe there was an error
3. **Appeal to Environment Court** - Within 15 working days (expensive and time-consuming)
4. **Consider alternative approaches** - Different design or location

---

### Q: Can I talk to a planner before applying?

**A: Yes - pre-application meetings are recommended for**:
- Non-complying or discretionary activities
- Complex proposals
- Understanding plan rules
- Discussing likely concerns
- Getting preliminary feedback

Click **"Book Pre-Application Meeting"** in the application form or contact council directly.

---

### Q: What happens after I submit?

**Processing steps**:
1. **Acknowledgment**: Council confirms receipt (5 working days)
2. **Completeness check**: Council reviews if all information provided
3. **RFI (if needed)**: Council requests additional information
4. **Notification decision**: Council decides if notification required
5. **Assessment**: Planner assesses against RMA and district plan
6. **Decision**: Grant with conditions, grant without conditions, or decline
7. **Notification**: Decision sent to you (and submitters if notified)

---

### Q: When does my consent expire?

**A: Depends on consent type**:
- **Land use consents**: Typically 5 years to give effect (start the activity)
- **Subdivision consents**: Usually 3-5 years to submit survey plan
- **Discharge/water permits**: Specific duration (5-35 years)

Check your consent decision notice for specific lapse dates.
